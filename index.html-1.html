PAYG Income Assessment

Complete reference guide for loan processors

What is PAYG?

PAYG (Pay As You Go) refers to individuals who earn income as a salaried employee — tax is withheld by the employer before the employee receives their wage.

✔ PAYG

Works under someone else. Tax is deducted by employer. Receives a payslip. Examples: nurses, teachers, engineers employed by a company.

⚙ Self Employed

Runs their own business. Responsible for own tax. PAYG employees may work under a self-employed person.

📋 In Australian mortgage processing, there are two income types: PAYG and Self-Employed. Each uses different documents and assessment methods.

Types of PAYG Employment

Type Hours Leave Entitlements Key Documents Lender Treatment
Full Time 38 hrs/week (standard). Fixed and guaranteed. Annual leave (4 wks), sick leave, long service, public holidays — all paid. 2 recent payslips + employment letter or last 2 years' tax returns Most stable. 100% of base income accepted.
Part Time Less than 38 hrs/week but fixed/consistent hours per contract. Same entitlements as full-time, pro-rated to hours worked. 2 payslips showing consistent hours + letter confirming ongoing employment Base income accepted at contracted hours. Must show consistency.
Casual No guaranteed hours. Varies week to week. No paid leave. Receives casual loading (~25%) in lieu of entitlements. 12 months' payslips + income statement often required. Higher risk — 12 months same role typically required. YTD annualised.
Contract Defined by contract — may be full-time hours but fixed term. Depends on contract type. Some entitlements if deemed employee. Copy of contract + payslips + renewal history Lenders assess renewal likelihood. May require 2 years history.
⚠ For casual employees, lenders require at least 12 months in the same role/industry. Income statement required if first 3 months of current financial year or discrepancy in payslips.

Income Statement

What is it?

An Income Statement (formerly Payment Summary / Group Certificate) is an official ATO document summarising an employee's total gross income and tax withheld for a financial year (1 July – 30 June). Provided via Single Touch Payroll (STP) and accessible through myGov → ATO.

ℹ Every employer submits STP data differently — layout and categories vary between organisations. However, the ATO income statement format is standardised once submitted.
Why and When Do We Use It?
📅
Start of Financial Year

Required for first 3 months of new FY (July–Sept). Payslips won't reflect a full year yet — prior year income statement used to verify income.

🔀
Casual Employees

Required to verify 12-month income continuity and calculate annualised income, since hours/income vary significantly.

⚠️
Payslip Discrepancy

If payslips show unusual figures or missing YTD, income statement is used as cross-reference to verify accuracy.

Where to Generate an Income Statement

Official ATO mobile app — same data as myGov

Accountant or registered tax agent can access via ATO portal
Employer
Employer must provide payment summary if not using STP

Deductions in a Pay Slip

Pre-Tax Deductions

Deducted before income tax is calculated. Reduces taxable income.

  • Salary sacrifice (super contributions)
  • Salary sacrifice (novated car lease)
  • Salary sacrifice (laptop / equipment)
  • Union fees (sometimes)
  • Workplace giving / charity
Post-Tax Deductions

Deducted after income tax is calculated. Does not reduce taxable income.

  • Private health insurance
  • Life insurance / income protection
  • Superannuation (above SG rate)
  • Employee share scheme
  • Employer loan repayments
  • Child support (court-ordered)

Deduction Assessment Rules

Deduction TypeActionYTD to UseReason
Voluntary IGNORE — do not deduct from income Use GROSS YTD Client chose to make the deduction; not a liability reducing income capacity
Mandatory / Non-Voluntary DEDUCT from base income Use TAXABLE YTD Court-ordered or compulsory; reduces true available income
Mandatory (annualised method) Annualise deduction first, then deduct from gross YTD Gross YTD Captures full-year impact even if deduction started mid-year
⚠ Examples of non-voluntary (mandatory) deductions: child support orders, garnishee orders, court-mandated repayments. These must be deducted from income before serviceability assessment.

YTD — Year to Date

What is YTD?

YTD (Year to Date) is the cumulative total of income (or deductions) from the start of the financial year (1 July) to the current payslip date. Used to annualise income where pay is variable — particularly for casual workers or employees with variable pay components.

YTD Annualisation Formula — Days Method
Annual Income = (YTD Gross ÷ Days Worked) × 365
Days Worked = Number of days from 1 July to payslip date
YTD Annualisation Formula — Pay Period Method
Annual Income = (YTD ÷ Pay Periods Elapsed) × Total Pay Periods in Year
E.g. Fortnightly: YTD ÷ 10 × 26 (if 10 fortnights have passed)
PAYG TypeUse YTD?Gross or Taxable?Reason
Full Time (fixed salary) Usually not — base salary × 52 sufficient — Stable; YTD is cross-check only
Part Time Yes, if hours vary Gross (if only voluntary deductions) Hours may differ per period
Casual Yes — always annualise from YTD Gross YTD or taxable if mandatory deductions No contracted hours; YTD is only reliable measure
Contract Yes, if pay varies Gross or taxable depending on deductions Contract terms may change between periods

Base Income vs Non-Base Income

Base Income

Regular, guaranteed income from employment. Reflects the contracted/consistent earnings the borrower reliably receives.

  • Base salary / wages
  • Contracted hours × base rate
  • Recurring allowances (car, travel, tools)
  • Regular leave loading
Full Time
Annual Base = Base Rate × Base Hours × 52
Part Time
Annual Base = (Contracted Hrs ÷ Wk) × Rate × 52
Non-Base Income

Variable income earned above and beyond the base. Not guaranteed. Lenders apply income shading before including.

  • Overtime
  • Commission
  • Bonus / incentive pay
  • Non-recurring allowances
Non-Base Annual Calculation
Non-Base (Annual) = YTD Non-Base ÷ Months × 12
Then apply lender's shading % before adding to servicing
ℹ Base income is accepted at 100% of the calculated annual figure. Non-base income is shaded (reduced) before use in serviceability. See the Income Shading tab.

Income Shading for Non-Base Income

What is Income Shading?

Income shading is the practice where lenders accept only a percentage of non-base income (overtime, commission, bonus) in serviceability calculations. Since this income is variable and not guaranteed, lenders apply a discount to reduce lending risk.

Why Do Lenders Apply Income Shading?
📉
Income Volatility

Overtime and bonuses can disappear overnight — especially during downturns, restructures, or role changes.

🏦
Prudent Lending

APRA guidelines require lenders to assess borrowers' repayment ability under stress scenarios.

🔁
Continuity Risk

Past non-base income does not guarantee future earnings. Shading creates a conservative buffer.

Typical Income Shading Rates (Common Industry Range)
